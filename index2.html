Following its conquering of Harvard, the Lions’ second win in a row, Columbia was riding high. Its next game represented a break from Ivy play; the Lions would instead take on Lehigh University.

Lehigh was a considerable opponent on a winning streak of its own. Against Columbia, that streak would continue. The Engineers dominated on the ground, beating Columbia at its own game to take home a 14-7 win.

“The ironically perverse lady who controls the fortunes of football gave the Columbia Lion a cruel slap in the face in the second half of Saturday’s contest against Lehigh after caressing him and leading him on during the first,” Spectator writer Barry Landau wrote.

“Columbia deserved to lose,” Waldbaum wrote. “Its line was outplayed and the entire team was outhustled by a fired-up band of Pennsylvanians determined to show up the New Yorkers.”

Had the Lions already peaked? Was the Lehigh game a sign of things to come?

“I just think we played a bad game,” Toner said. “No one can be singled out for under-par play. The whole team was under-par.”

The Lions would have a week to feel “ashamed,” as Strauss thought they should, before they got another chance to take on the Ivy League.

BROWN

At this point in the season, the race for the Ivy title was tightly contested. Princeton, sitting at the top of the table with a 3-0 record, was rolling. Columbia, at 3-1, trailed closely behind the Tigers, with Yale, Dartmouth, and Harvard hot on the Lions’ tails.

Cornell appeared to be an easy opponent on paper. Plagued by injuries, the Big Red was winless in the Ancient Eight. However, the previous week, Cornell put up a strong fight against Princeton, while Columbia played one of its worst games of the year against Lehigh. Moreover, Warren’s status was still in question after he suffered a bruised left knee in the game against Harvard.

Columbia was still a heavy favorite, and Cornell head coach Tom Harp knew that: “It’s been a nightmare of a season,” he said bluntly. “We’ll be outmanned by Columbia on Saturday.”

The Lions seemed to have taken the Lehigh loss to heart and dominated most of the game. Less than three minutes in, O’Connor ran 33 yards to the end zone to cap off a 74-yard drive. However, Cornell responded later in the quarter, taking the lead 7-6 on a 35-yard touchdown pass. Later in the half, Cornell punter Dave McKelvey booted it deep into Columbia territory, and Haggerty stunned the Ithaca crowd by returning the punt 84 yards—the longest return in Columbia history—to regain the lead for the Lions.

“The run did more than set a record; it turned the tide against a scrappy Cornell squad that had overcome an early Columbia touchdown to take a 7-6 lead,” Spectator writer Ira Youdovin wrote. “No psychiatrist was necessary to diagnose the demoralizing effect Haggerty’s stunner had on the home side.”

After the floodgates opened, Columbia could not be stopped. The Lions marched to a 35-7 victory as Haggerty completed two marvelous touchdown runs from 47 and 64 yards out.

Haggerty’s record-breaking performance was one for the ages. In addition to his 85-yard return record, Haggerty set Columbia records for yards from scrimmage with 148, and points against an Ivy opponent with 18. Despite the heavy workload they had been taking on, Haggerty and his fellow running backs showed no signs of slowing down.

Cornell’s injury-ridden 11 had not tested the Lions, but the Light Blue still trailed Princeton in the standings. What’s more, Columbia would face down a formidable opponent the next week: Dartmouth.

CORNELL

Columbia, Dartmouth, and Harvard had all lost only one Ivy game, behind only undefeated Princeton. For this reason, beating Dartmouth was of paramount importance for the Lions.

Columbia would be playing an Ivy opponent in front of its home crowd for the first time in over a month, drawing over 25,000 spectators in its largest crowd yet that season. The Lions were out for blood, and they did not fail, securing a dominant 35-14 victory.

The defense shined in this one. Day, continuing his marvelous season on both sides of the ball, swatted Dartmouth’s Tom Spangenberg’s pass on the Dartmouth 10, which wide receiver Dick Hassan, CC ’62, fell on in the end zone to give Columbia the first points of the game. Then, on the very first play after the ensuing kickoff, Columbia forced a fumble, recovered the ball, and eventually took it to the end zone yet again as Vasell found Hassan for six.

Columbia shored up its lead in the third quarter, when Haggerty ran the show. On the Lions’ opening drive of the second half, Haggerty carried the ball 13 times for 46 yards, capping the drive off with a two-yard touchdown run. To close out the third, he did nearly the exact same thing, carrying the ball 10 more times for 45 yards and a touchdown to give Columbia a 20-point lead.

Warren put the icing on the cake by breaking away for a 43-yard score with under 2 minutes left in regulation. It was Columbia’s fifth touchdown of the game, but something bigger had happened. Just moments before, Harvard had beaten Princeton 9-7, meaning that Harvard, Columbia, and Princeton would hold a three-way tie for first place in the Ivy League as soon as the clock read zero.

As Columbia only had one Ivy game remaining, a win the following week against Penn would guarantee co-champion status at the minimum. Princeton had two games remaining, one against Yale and the other against Dartmouth, and Harvard still had to play Brown and Yale. So in order to become the sole possessors of the Ivy League trophy, the Lions would need some help. “I’d like to see you beat Princeton,” Donelli told Dartmouth coach Bob Black after the game.

Something had changed. An excitement now surrounded Columbia football. Donelli began to receive telegrams from Columbia alumni who now “feel that it’s all possible. This was an attitude that was previously absent at Columbia,” Donelli said. After five straight losing seasons, Buff Donelli was a winner again.

On campus, there was a new sense of school spirit shared among students, which student board chairman Dave Tucker, CC ’62, described as “tremendous.”

“The interest on campus grew from week to week,” Waldbaum said.

The buzz was not only important for the football team, though. “This means a great deal to me,” said Dave Tomkins, CC ’62, the chief drum beater at pep rallies. “I’ve taken a lot of ribbing from my friends at other Ivy League schools in the past. I’m proud as the devil.”

The Ancient Eight crown was right there for the Lions. One more win was all it would take.

PRINCETON

At 5-1, the ball was in the Lions’ court. If they beat Penn, they were guaranteed a tie for first place at the very least; a loss would make their position precarious at best.

Penn, Ivy League champions only two years earlier, had a significant edge in the all-time matchup. Columbia had not beaten Penn at home since 1934. The Quakers had beaten Columbia by a combined score of 66-6 in 1958 and 1959, and no Penn team had ever lost to Columbia in consecutive years.

However, “the pendulum [had] swung” in Columbia’s favor, Sherman observed. In 1960, Columbia took down Penn for the first time in 23 years. Now, the Lions sat at the top of the Ancient Eight, while the Quakers found themselves in sixth place, a region of the standings Columbia was used to inhabiting.

Despite the changing of the guard, Penn head coach John Stiegman’s confidence did not falter. “Sure, we’ll be up for Columbia,” he said. In preparation, Stiegman planned to move five reserves into the starting lineup to rejuvenate his squad, a decision that puzzled Donelli.

“Who are they?” he asked.

While beating Penn was their main goal, the Lions were also looking for some help from Yale, which was set to play Princeton that week. “I guess that everyone at Columbia must be wearing both light and dark blue clothes this week,” Olivar joked.

Yale did not mind helping the Lions out. “If we don’t win it will be nice if one of the strangers to the Ivy title does,” Olivar added. “Princeton has won before.”

The stage was set. As long as the Lions handled their business in front of their home crowd, history would be made. If they failed, then it would be up to the football gods to decide their fate.

With their squad nearly at full strength, Columbia left nothing up to chance, blowing Penn out of the water 37-6.

With Columbia up 3-0, thanks to a field goal from O’Connor early in the first quarter, the Quakers were forced to punt from their own end zone as a Warren punt cornered them in their own territory. The Penn punt would not go far. Hassan broke through a line of Quakers and smothered the punt, which Black caught in the end zone to give the Lions’ their first touchdown.

Image courtesy of Columbia Athletics

In the second quarter, Columbia went back to its bread and butter. The Lions’ three primary backs rushed 10 times in a drive as Warren scored to increase the lead to 17. Before the close of the half, Dick Sakala, CC ’64, would run it in from 40 yards on a quarterback draw, making the score 23-0. The dominance continued in the second half: Haggerty, who had a dazzling 68-yard run in the third quarter, scored his 10th touchdown of the year, before Vasell converted on a quarterback sneak to conclude the scoring for the Lions.

Time expired. It was over; the job was finally finished. The Lions did the unthinkable. They were champions.

Image courtesy of Columbia Athletics

“We didn’t think at the beginning that we could win the championship,” Donelli said.

“This is very much a team.”

Lions—athletes, coaches, students, and administrators—were filled with jubilation. When asked how he felt, athletic director Ralph Furey responded, “Pretty good. Yes, wonderful; I think that you can see from the people around here that this has been a shot in the arm for the entire Columbia family, and it will spur our other teams on.”

Now, all the Lions had to do was wait.

PENN

In week eight, Harvard beat a winless Brown team, boosting its record to 5-1. The Crimson’s final matchup was against Yale, which had failed to beat Princeton in week eight. Ahead of the game, Vasell, Campbell, and Warren sent the Bulldogs a telegram wishing them luck against Harvard. However, the Lions’ efforts to boost their temporary allies did not bear fruit as Harvard shut Yale out 27-0. Yale came up short for the Lions once again.

Columbia lost to Rutgers 32-19 that week, but the game did not count toward the Ivy League standings.

After beating Yale, Princeton lost to Dartmouth in its season finale 24-6. That meant that Columbia and Harvard would share the title as each held a 6-1 record. Meanwhile, the Tigers’ loss bumped them to 5-2, putting them out of title qualification.

According to Vasell, Donelli argued to the coaches committee that the Lions should be the sole owners of the title since they had beaten Harvard earlier in the year. However, it was not to be—Columbia and Harvard were co-champions. Despite the potential for ill will because of the decision, the team was not bitter, according to Vasell.

“We were happy to be champions.”
