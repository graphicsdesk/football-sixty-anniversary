With their preseason victory over Bucknell under their belts, the Lions traveled to Providence, Rhode Island, to take on the Brown Bruins in their Ivy League opener on Sept. 30. Brown had a young, inexperienced squad, with only five seniors on its 53-man roster. Columbia, meanwhile, had 17 returning lettermen, the most in the Ivy League.

The Lions had dominated the Bruins 37-0 the year prior. This time, they one-upped that performance, putting on an offensive clinic in Providence. It only took Columbia’s second touchdown, a 56-yard connection between Vasell and Warren, to “[break] the spirit of the Providence team,” according to Spectator writer Conrad Sherman.

Image courtesy of Columbia Athletics

The Lions proceeded to score on each of their next two possessions and play all 43 of their healthy players. Columbia rushed for 295 yards, led by the three-headed monster of Haggerty, O’Connor, and Warren, who scored three touchdowns. By the time all was said and done, the scoreboard read “Columbia 50 Brown 0”—the Light Blue’s largest victory, ever.

“It appeared to all the New Englanders that Buff Donelli, like the Norse God Thor, had hurled a lightning [sic] bolt at their fair city,” Waldbaum wrote after the game.

Winning in such dominant fashion against Brown was certainly a confidence boost for the Lions, but they knew that they had bigger fish to fry—or rather, tigers to cook.

“Sure we were looking forward to next week’s game against Princeton,” Warren said. “We want to win that game more than anything else in the world. But the coaches reminded us that we had a job to do here today, and do it we did. Actually, we all figured that Brown would be a lot tougher than it was.”

Donelli just had one thing to say to his team: “Stay healthy. There is a long season ahead.”

With that, the Light Blue returned home and set its eyes on its next challenger, the Princeton Tigers.

Columbia victories against Princeton were rare—the last time the Lions beat the Tigers had been in 1945, when first-years Gene Rossides and Lou Kusserow led the Light Blue past the Tigers 32-7. Columbia victories against Princeton at home were even rarer: The last time Princeton lost at Baker Field was in 1932.

To make matters worse, the Lions had not been able to muster a single point against Princeton in three years. However, hopes were high this year: Columbia went into the game as the favorite, according to the New York Times. Princeton head coach Dick Colman even praised the offensive “quickness” and “tremendous” defense of the Light Blue ahead of their week two matchup.

This was a big game for the Lions. Not only would a victory break the Lions’ losing streak, it would also crown their Homecoming game. A pep rally was held to stir up school spirit that week. “We will try to acquire some of the pride in athletics which exists at Princeton,” an announcement read. “The Columbia varsity football team needs your support Friday night.”

Over 23,000 gathered at Baker Field on a game day marked by boiling heat. Despite the Lions’ historic woes against the Tigers, Columbia was confident it could vanquish its “perennial tormenters,” as Spectator writer Alan Willen dubbed Princeton.

With alumni and students alike cheering them on, the Lions struck first. On Columbia’s opening drive, O’Connor broke out for a 46-yard touchdown run, putting the Lions on the scoreboard.

The Lions did not relent as O’Connor punched in another score to put the Lions up 14-0. At this point, Donelli put the backups in. The crowd buzzed with excitement at the thought of Columbia finally breaking the curse.

But unlike the previous week’s opponents, the Tigers were not done fighting just yet. On the last play of the first quarter, Princeton’s Hugh Macmillan connected with Jim Rockenbach at their own 35. Rockenbach took it the remaining 65 yards, making it a one-possession game.

The momentum shifted. The Princetonians in the crowd were now the primary noise-makers.

The second unit did not get another chance to play after this defensive lapse. In the second period, Rockenbach took the ball 28 yards for his second score. Princeton converted on the ensuing two-point attempt to take the lead 15-14.

In the second half, Warren, who ended the day with 102 yards on the ground, punched in a touchdown to regain the lead 20-15. The Tigers responded with a 12-play, 75-yard drive that ended with a score for Princeton. Then Princeton’s Greg Riley put the icing on the cake in the fourth quarter with an 87-yard touchdown run.

It was a physically grueling game—due to the heat, Warren lost 16 pounds, and Black lost 20—and a devastating loss for the Lions, who had hoped they could finally take Princeton down. Warren, who played through a nose injury, called it “probably the most disappointing game I ever played in.”

Still, there were many positive takeaways. “I feel better about this team’s ability than I did after the Brown game,” Donelli said following the loss. “They showed that they could run, that they could pass, and that they could defend against a real good football team.”

The Lions would have to forget their disappointment and put their sights on their next challenger—Yale, the reigning Ivy League champion.

The Bulldogs, fresh off an 18-0 win over Connecticut, were riding an 11-game winning streak, the longest in the nation. Nevertheless, Yale head coach Jordan Olivar was rather pessimistic for a man whose team had not lost a game in nearly two years.

“There’s no doubt that the ‘experts’ were wrong about our team. There is no comparison between this squad and last year’s,” Olivar said.

Though Yale’s 1960 team went undefeated and ended the season as the No. 14 team in the nation according to The Associated Press, after losing 10 starters, the new Yale team bore little resemblance to the previous year’s. As Olivar put it: “We have no depth.”

However, Yale had a talented quarterback in Bill Leckonby and a powerful running game built around fullback Ted Hard. In addition, Columbia would be without Haggerty, who suffered a charley horse against Princeton. While the Bulldogs may not have been as strong as they were the previous year, no one expected them to be a walk in the park.

“Much like a middle-aged banker, Yale is solid, formidable, and conservative,” Spectator writer Reed Strauss wrote.

Joining another crowd of over 23,000, the Light Blue traveled to the Yale Bowl in hopes of ending the Bulldogs’ streak. However, the Bulldogs started strong, as Hard and fellow fullback Dick Niglio came out of the gates with a fast attack. The duo combined for 26 yards on four consecutive plays before a penalty stifled the Yale offense. At the 7:30 mark of the first quarter, Al Butts, CC ’64, picked off Leckonby’s pass, but the Lions could not convert on the interception.

Image courtesy of Columbia Athletics

Butts, a sophomore thrust into the starting lineup in the wake of Haggerty’s injury, would become the game’s unexpected hero. In the second quarter, Butts ran to the end zone for the game’s first and only touchdown. He then scored himself on the ensuing two-point conversion.

Neither offense was able to gain an edge from that point on. Vasell threw two interceptions, but Yale could not capitalize. Midway through the fourth quarter, Butts intercepted another Yale pass to ice the game. O’Connor later kicked a field goal to end the scoring at 11-0.

Butts had been a reliable defensive player in the past, but even Donelli did not expect his heroics: He ended the game with 66 yards, two key interceptions, and the game’s only touchdown. His performance earned him Ivy League Back of the Week honors.

After the previous week’s gut-punching loss to Princeton, snapping the Bulldogs’ winning streak was rejuvenating for the Light Blue—and other Columbians.

After the victory, the Columbia University Marching Band marched to “Roar, Lion, Roar” on Yale’s campus. In retaliation, a group of Yale supporters attacked the band and stole its glockenspiel. X-rays later revealed that one Columbia tuba player left the scene with a bruised bone and torn leg muscles. Regardless of the damage, “the Blue never broke formation, and left the Eli campus proudly playing ‘Who Owns New York?’” Spectator writer Peter Trooboff, CC ’64, reported. Columbia did what would have been unthinkable only months before.

“If we beat Yale we have a good chance to beat anyone in the league,” Donelli said prior to the game. Next, Columbia would test its luck against Harvard.

The Lions traveled to Cambridge to avenge an injustice: a year-old Homecoming robbery. One year earlier, Harvard, down 7-6 at Columbia’s Homecoming, went for a go-ahead two-point conversion, which was deemed successful by the umpire, giving the Crimson an 8-7 victory. An NBC film later revealed that Harvard had not actually converted and that the Crimson ballcarrier was actually stopped before crossing the plane.

The Lions were looking to right last year’s wrong in what turned out to be a pivotal Ivy League game. Harvard was without seven players, including starting quarterback Ted Halaby. The Lions, meanwhile, would be welcoming back Haggerty after he sat out against Princeton.

Much like Olivar the week before, Harvard head coach John Yovicsin was not particularly confident in his team: “I don’t know if the boys will be up for the game. I wish I could read their minds.”

The first quarter saw little action, with neither team scoring, but that did not last long. In the second quarter, O’Connor broke through the Harvard line from six yards out to open up the scoring. Shortly after, Haggerty punched one in of his own from the Harvard nine after the Crimson had turned it over off of a high snap on a fourth down punt attempt.

Harvard quickly responded by scoring on a fourth down passing play after junior Hank Hatch returned Warren’s kick-off 66 yards and put the Crimson in great field position. Harvard fans roared in approval.

Columbia quickly silenced the Cambridge crowd. This time, it was Warren’s turn—before the end of the half, he ran it in for six more points.

The Crimson controlled the third quarter, scoring again to narrow the Columbia lead down to 20-14. However, Harvard would make another fatal error. In the fourth, Harvard quarterback Mike Bassett threw a lateral pass, only for it to be intercepted by the Light Blue. Haggerty proceeded to score his second touchdown of the day on a one-yard drive, sealing the deal 26-14.

The Lions’ three-headed running back crew stuffed the stat sheet. On 60 carries, Warren, Haggerty, and O’Connor combined for 244 yards and scored all four of Columbia’s touchdowns.

Once again, Columbia won handily, and once again, the CUMB made its presence felt. The Blue Band entered Cambridge with an 8-foot-4-inch drum made of paper and wood, mocking Harvard’s famous 6-foot-6-inch drum. Five of Harvard’s drummers tore apart the imitation drum, and 50 Columbia fans rushed the Harvard band and the giant drum in response but failed to do any damage.

The win marked the Lions’ third in Ivy League play and the first time the team had won consecutive Ivy League games in a decade. A conference title was well within reach, but Donelli kept a level head. “Let’s not look ahead too far,” he said. “But we’re still in the ball game, I guess.”
